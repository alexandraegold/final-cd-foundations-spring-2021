THE CREATURES

The Samodiva

PRONUNCIATION

THE samodiva is described as “unearthly beautiful and eternally young.” They spend their time in forests and dark caves near water sources. They stay close to the water, as they love to bathe and dance in the moonlight. It is here that they seduce traveling men or shepherds with their beautiful music—enticing them to join the dance, and deserting them in the morning to die of exhaustion.

ORIGINS: SLAVIC FOLKLORE

The Nymph

PRONUNCIATION

THE nymph is “a blanket term encompassing female spirits of wild nature.” Nymphs are generally associated with healing, often overseeing “human reproduction overseeing fertility, pregnancy, childbirth, child care, and children” but they can also “express their displeasure by inflicting bad luck, infertility, impotence, and mental disorders, including insanity, depression, and the inability to find joy."

ORIGINS: GREEK AND ROMAN MYTHOLOGY

The Banshee

PRONUNCIATION

THE banshee is a spirit considered to be a sign of death. Banshees are most frequently either depicted as “crouching hags with horrible wrinkly faces” or “beautiful, ethereal young women." Folklore says that she will cry her shrill, sorrowful moan in the nights before a death occurs.

ORIGINS: CELTIC FOLKLORE

The Rusalka

PRONUNCIATION

THE rusalka is said to be “made” if a young woman dies a particularly violent death or suicide related to water. She tempts people, especially young men, with her seductive voice or good looks. She then traps him and pulls him under the water. In “folklore stories of heroes, the Rusalka often represents a ‘test’ and if abused or if the hero fails the test he will be cast into a watery grave.”

ORIGINS: SLAVIC FOLKLORE

The Huldra

PRONUNCIATION

THE huldra is a beautiful female creature from Norse folklore. She is said to have long blond hair but the tail of a cow. Legend says that she would seduce young unmarried men and take them into the mountains, where she would not let them go unless they would marry her. If a man did marry her she would turn into an ugly woman.

ORIGINS: SCANDINAVIAN FOLKLORE

THE BEAUTIFUL AND THE EVIL

THE HISTORY

The creatures on this page are all a part of the "beautiful but evil" stereotype for women. They often appear as ethereal and young, but ultimately use their alluring nature to cause evil to mankind — especially to passerby men. Throughout popular mythology "the themes of female disobedience and curiosity rear their problematic heads" Women are depicted as incapable of containing their wicked tendencies.
